# Decision Cases

## 1. Special guest repricing
When a pre-event update strongly changes the likely event frame, reprice the entire market surface immediately rather than just nudging one strike.

## 2. Late disciplined entry
In fast markets, waiting until close to the event can improve information quality while still preserving fills.

## 3. Ballroom / news-driven steam
A trade can be good at 39-40 cents and no longer good at 75 cents, even if the underlying narrative still looks live.

## 4. Partial trigger / dispute risk
If the event is progressing toward a trigger but not fully clean yet, there can be temporary edge — but only with explicit dispute awareness.

## 5. Go-live liquidity collapse
Spreads and stale-order risk increase sharply once the event starts. Go-live execution is a different game from pre-event execution.

## 6. Q&A path dependency
Some strikes are only really alive if Q&A happens. Misreading Q&A probability can produce major mispricing.

## 7. Small portfolio microstructure edge
With small size, obvious bad quotes and live dislocations can be easier and cleaner edge than heroic event prediction.

## 8. Drug announcement comparables
When the event directly names counterparties and recent correlated events repeatedly named those entities, high-probability yeses can still be undervalued.

## 9. Bond + mentions portfolio split
Use high-probability mispriced contracts for compounding while keeping separate risk budget for volatile mention-market trades.

## 10. Segmented recurring-market model
If a recurring mention market keeps “running bad,” do not assume variance first. Check whether your historical bucket is too broad and needs segmentation by booth, network, format, or game state.

## 11. Copy-trading premium trap
A sharp’s entry and your entry are different trades once the number has moved. Copying the thesis after the move without rebuilding fair value is usually just paying a premium.

## 12. Field repricing after informed flow
In performer-style or field markets, informed flow into a few names should reprice the untouched names too. The edge is often in the rest of the field, not only the pumped contracts.

## 13. Setup-label overreaction
Open press / closed press cues matter, but the market often turns them into fake certainty. Rebuild the path from actual event structure and current context.

## 14. Late-path still alive
Some words are structurally late-path words. If they are unsaid in prepared remarks, that may mean nothing if the real path is later Q&A or detour-driven.

## 15. Garbage-time language drift
In announcer markets, dead-game / late-game conditions can change the language mix. What looks like random variance may actually be a predictable phase shift in chatter.
